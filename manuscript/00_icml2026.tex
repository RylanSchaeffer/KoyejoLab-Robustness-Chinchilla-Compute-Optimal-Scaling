\begin{abstract}
\citet{hoffman2022chinchilla} introduced compute-optimal scaling, providing the ``Standard Model'' for scaling frontier language models.
However, recent concerns regarding wide confidence intervals and inconsistent results have left the field uncertain if Chinchilla’s prescriptions remain reliable. We begin by uncovering a critical specification error: the model parameters central to the original analyses were ambiguous, with definitional discrepancies as high as 15.2\%.
We find that, perhaps surprisingly, which model parameters are used for the analyses do not meaningfully affect key results: the scaling law estimates and the compute-optimal tokens-to-parameter ratio. Indeed, under one interpretation, the ratio becomes more constant with the target compute budget.
By systematically perturbing model parameters in four structured ways, we find that key results withstand sizable distortions. Critically, we show that additive specification errors (e.g., miscounting embeddings) mathematically mimic "broken" scaling laws—offering a diagnostic framework to distinguish between functional breakdown and simple accounting artifacts. Our findings offer renewed confidence in Chinchilla as a durable guide for scaling modern models.
\end{abstract}
